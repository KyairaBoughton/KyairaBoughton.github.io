Balancing Scales: Exploring Job Compensation and Cost of Living Disparities

The summarized version


Author: Kyaira Boughton

Project Date: March 1st, 2024

Code Link: View in Google Codelab

Abstract

This research investigates the intricate relationship between job compensation and the cost of living, offering novel insights into the contemporary employment landscape. Focusing on millennials and recent graduates navigating the job market, the study employs a unique approach by evaluating compensation within the context of specific regions. Distinguishing itself from existing literature, the research utilizes a random forest regression to integrate geographical factors, shedding light on how location contributes to the compensation dynamics. The research leverages meticulously curated datasets, aligning with the 2023 US job market. Despite challenges in data processing and model training, the logistic regression models provide valuable categorization of job sectors and seniority levels. The study contributes to ongoing employment discussions, prompting further exploration of nuanced factors influencing compensation in the modern workforce.

Keywords: job compensation, cost of living, employment landscape

Introduction

In contemporary discourse, employment and compensation discussions, particularly among the younger generation, are widespread. The challenges of securing employment and concerns about relatively low salaries are persistent topics. This paper addresses the pertinent question of how individuals in the modern era are remunerated, focusing on substantiated data. Beyond job-seeking concerns, it examines economic disparities' broader implications for employee well-being. As millennials and recent graduates navigate the job market, this project evaluates job compensation in specific regions concerning the cost of living. Going beyond simplistic predictors, it explores the real-world impact on individuals' lives. While existing research has explored salary prediction models, with notable works such as Jackman and Reid's (2013) "Predicting Job Salaries from Text Descriptions," this essay distinguishes itself through its unique focus on the geographical aspect of job locations and an end goal not of prediction, but inference and analysis. Through this exploration, the essay aims to contribute novel insights into the intricate factors shaping modern job salaries.

Selecting the Data

The foundation of my research lies in two distinct datasets meticulously curated to unravel the intricacies of the United States job market in 2023. The primary source, the Kaggle job posting dataset, encompasses approximately 33,000 job postings extracted from the LinkedIn website. The allure of this dataset lies in its focus on the United States, aligning seamlessly with our research interests. The temporal relevance of the data, being from 2023, ensures a current and pertinent examination of the job market. From the multitude of columns available, we selected key features crucial to our analysis, including job descriptions, titles, skills, and salary details (maximum, median, and minimum). To ensure the robustness of our analysis, we excluded job postings outside the United States and removed entries lacking salary information. Given that our primary focus was on average or median salaries, we normalized job postings with salary ranges by averaging them. For jobs with hourly wages, we converted them to annual salaries by multiplying with 2080. Recognizing the limitation of this approach, we acknowledge a potential overestimation of average salaries. Furthermore, we refined the dataset by eliminating entries with a generic location like "United States" without specifying a state or city. This meticulous curation resulted in a dataset comprising around 12,500 job postings, setting the stage for our subsequent analyses. The integration of geocoding API facilitated harmonization of job locations across datasets.

The second dataset, abbreviated as COL (Cost of Living), consists of cost of living indices for approximately 500 US cities in 2021. Each entry includes city, state, and the cost of living index. Given the importance of comparing purchasing power, we multiplied the cost of living index by the average yearly expenditure for 2020 ($61,334) and 2023 ($72,967). This enhancement enables meaningful cross- year comparisons, enriching the dataset's utility. Similar to the job dataset, geocoding API was employed to align city locations consistently.

Training Models for Filtration

In this research, logistic regression was employed as the primary modeling approach to classify job sectors and seniority levels within the dataset. The rationale behind choosing logistic regression lies in its suitability for categorical prediction, precisely aligned with the nature of the research question aiming to discern potential associations between job factors and higher wages or superior job opportunities within specific geographic regions. Logistic regression is well-established for categorical outcome prediction and thus was deemed apt for the task at hand. The dataset, comprising a total of 10,500 entries once non-geocodable jobs were removed, underwent a rigorous labeling process involving logistic regression for job sectors and seniority levels. To facilitate model training and testing, a 70/30 train-test split was implemented on a final dataset containing 300 entries that I hand labeled. Results

The results of the logistic regression models for job sectors and seniority levels provide valuable insights into the predictive capabilities of the trained models. The accuracy scores obtained during the training process serve as crucial metrics for evaluating the models' performance. The logistic regression model for job sectors achieved an accuracy score of 0.33, indicating a lower predictive performance in categorizing job sectors. In contrast, the model predicting job seniority levels exhibited a more favorable accuracy score of 0.74, signifying a higher degree of precision in predicting seniority.

Conclusion

This research delves into a comparative analysis of the cost of living across different locations, contributing valuable insights to the field of employment research and opportunities. The study explores a potential avenue for future researchers, suggesting ways to enhance categorization within job data for more nuanced analysis. The outlined method is poised to aid in creating more specific job information categories. The findings challenge the perception of job salaries falling below the cost of living in specific areas. However, it is crucial to note that the dataset had several limitations that may have affected the effectiveness of this iteration of this research project.
